Note on Z scores and normalising data

This will come up later with one of our UN data columns.

We discussed Z scores in the lecture briefly - a Z score for a single value is its distance from the mean in standard deviations. We find this by (a) subtracting the mean from each observation and (b) dividing by the standard deviation. For our M&Ms guesses, this looks like the following:

guesses <- c(675,972,572,316,1500,2500,700,2500,387,310,750,507,345)

#Z score: subtract mean of all guesses from each guess, then divide the lot by the standard deviation
zscores <- (guesses - mean(guesses))/sd(guesses)

These Z scorees look like this:

Finding Z scores for a whole vector like this has the effect of standardising or scaling the variable. This means that, for all Z score values together:

  • The mean will zero (or very close to)
  • The standard deviation of the whole lot will be one (or very close to)
mean(zscores)
## [1] -0.000000000000000006405133
sd(zscores)
## [1] 1

So, we have scaled the data so that its values match what the standard deviation values in a normal distribution are. Meaning - the new, adjusted values will match the standard deviation scale on the bottom of this (assuming the data is normally distributed):

This normalising or scaling is often used for standardising scores, and for other statistical purposes. Well see it crop up a few times - including with the data below.

Note: the R function scale achieves the same thing as manually creating our Z scores (if we use its defaults):

zscores2 <- scale(guesses)

mean(zscores2)
## [1] -0.00000000000000003682117
sd(zscores2)
## [1] 1

The data

Like legislators in the US Congress, the member states of the United Nations (UN) are politically divided on many issues such as trade, nuclear disarmament, and human rights. During the Cold War, countries in the UN General Assembly tended to split into two factions: one led by the capitalist United States and the other by the communist Soviet Union.

We will analyze how states ideological positions, as captured by their votes on UN resolutions, have changed since the fall of communism.

This exercise is based on Michael A. Bailey, Anton Strezhnev, and Erik Voeten. Estimating Dynamic State Preferences from United Nations Voting Data. Journal of Conflict Resolution, August 2015.

In that paper, they estimate state ideal points from 1946 to 2012 on a single dimension that reflects state positions toward the US-led liberal order. (More on what ideal points are shortly.)

The data we downloaded to the data folder is called unvoting.csv and the variables are:

Name Description
CountryName The name of the country
idealpoint Its estimated ideal point
Year The year for which the ideal point is estimated
PctAgreeUS The percentage of votes that agree with the US on the same issue
PctAgreeRUSSIA The percentage of votes that agree with Russia/the Soviet Union on the same issue

In the analysis that follows, we measure state preferences in two ways. Note that the data for 1964 are missing due to the absence of roll call data.

First, we can use the percentage of votes by each country that coincide with votes on the same issue cast by the two major Cold War powers: the United States and the Soviet Union. For example, if a country voted for ten resolutions in 1992, and if its vote matched the United Statess vote on exactly six of these resolutions, the variable PctAgreeUS in 1992 would equal 60 for this country.

Second, we can also measure state preferences in terms of numerical ideal points.
These ideal points capture what international relations scholars have called countries liberalism on issues such as political freedom, democratization, and financial liberalization.

In this data, the ideal point is a single number, so just a one-dimensional scale, with more liberal countries at the top of the positive values. Its roughly equivalent to a left-right political spectrum scale.

This is a good summary of what ideal points are: blogs.iq.harvard.edu/ideal_points_1

The ideal point value captures this in one number: a legislator will vote in favor of a motion if it moves policy outcomes closer to their most preferred policy (represented by their ideal point).

The two measures are highly correlated, with larger (more liberal) ideal points corresponding to a higher percentage of votes that agree with the US.

Quick aside: missing values

In R, missing values are indicated by NA. So, for example, if we didnt know the final value in this vector, wed put:

x <- c(1,4,7,9,NA)

R handles missing values differently in different functions. Note what the behaviour of the mean function is:

mean(x)
## [1] NA

Because theres one NA in the data, the mean function just returns NA. However, we can tell the mean function to remove any missing values and just find the mean for the non-missing values:

mean(x, na.rm = TRUE)
## [1] 5.25

Take a look in the ?help file for the mean function. Notice that it says na.rm = FALSE - the help files show what the default for any argument is, if you dont override it.
